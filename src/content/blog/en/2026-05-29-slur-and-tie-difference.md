# Slurs and Ties — How Two Curves That Look the Same Mean Different Things

A piano student opens a beginner's score. In the second measure, there's a curved line connecting two notes of the same pitch. A few pages later, another curved line — this time connecting C, D, and E. They look identical. But the teacher says one is played by holding a single note, the other by playing several notes smoothly. Same drawing, completely different instructions.

That's the central confusion behind one of the most common ambiguities in music notation. Two curves, identical in shape, with entirely different meanings: the **slur** and the **tie**.

The confusion isn't trivial. A score that mixes ties and slurs across the same passage tells the performer two completely different things at once: how long the notes last, and how they're physically played. Mistaking one for the other doesn't just change the music — it can make a passage technically impossible. A piece written to be sustained turns into one full of unnecessary attacks. A phrase meant to flow becomes a series of repeated notes.

## What the Curve Connects

The fastest way to tell them apart is to look at what's at each end of the curve.

- Connects two notes of the **same pitch** → tie
- Connects two or more notes of **different pitches** → slur

That single rule resolves almost every case. A curve between two C's is a tie. A curve over C-D-E is a slur. The exception: not every curve between same-pitch notes is a tie. A repeated pitch embedded inside a larger melodic phrase can be drawn under a slur instead.

Both notations have existed since the seventeenth century. In the era of simple printing, the curve shape was borrowed for two distinct musical ideas, and the visual overlap stuck. Modern notation kept both meanings under the same drawn shape.

## The Tie — Adding Time

A tie indicates **note duration**. A quarter-note C tied to another quarter-note C is functionally a half-note C. The two written values combine; the note sounds once and is held through both.

This notation is most often used to carry a note across a barline. When a duration doesn't fit cleanly inside the meter of a single measure, the value is split and tied across the bar.

When playing a tied note, the finger strikes only the first instance. The second written note isn't struck again — its time is simply absorbed. Striking it twice turns the music into something else.

## The Slur — Grouping Expression

A slur indicates **how the notes are played**, not how long they last. Notes inside a slur are performed smoothly, without separation — what Italians call *legato*, "connected."

On piano, the finger transitions cleanly from one key to the next without lifting between attacks. On strings, several notes are played within a single bow stroke. On wind instruments and in singing, the slur defines a single breath. The technical execution depends on the instrument; the principle stays the same: **group the notes into one expressive unit**.

![Comparison diagram: ties, slurs, and ligatures — three different musical intentions sharing one curved shape.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Ties_slurs_and_ligature.svg/960px-Ties_slurs_and_ligature.svg.png)

For trained musicians, notes inside a slur are processed not as separate events but as **a single phrase**. In a 1989 study, Caroline Palmer analyzed pianists' performances and found that skilled players adjusted note timing, dynamics, and articulation according to the phrase markings printed in the score (Palmer, 1989). A slur is not decoration. It is a structural unit boundary.

## Edge Cases Where the Rule Bends

Real scores sometimes blur the distinction.

- **Same-pitch curve, no barline involved.** Could go either way. If one of the notes is part of a dotted figure, the curve often serves as a phrase mark rather than a duration tie.
- **A large slur containing repeated pitches.** The outer curve is a slur; the repeated pitches inside are played as separate notes unless they are additionally tied.
- **A slur with staccato dots inside.** That's *portato* — a middle expression between connected and detached. The notes are gently separated within a larger phrase.

The most reliable check: look at the pitches at the two ends of the curve. Same pitch → almost certainly a tie. Different pitches → almost certainly a slur.

Engraving conventions add a secondary visual clue. In well-engraved modern scores, ties tend to sit flatter and closer to the noteheads, hugging them, while slurs arch more dramatically to encompass several pitches. The convention isn't universal — composer autographs are far less consistent. Schubert's manuscripts, for example, often use sweeping curves where a modern edition would distinguish ties from phrase marks. Reading historical sources requires checking the pitch rule even more carefully.

Return to the two curves at the start. The first, connecting two C's: a tie. One note, held longer. The second, connecting C and E: a slur. Two notes, one breath. Different fingers, different breath, different musical intent.

Once those two curves separate cleanly in your reading, the staff becomes a richer document — carrying time information (note length) and expression information (how it's played) in the same drawn shape. Recognizing which is which takes about half a second. Reading the difference unlocks half of music's surface.
